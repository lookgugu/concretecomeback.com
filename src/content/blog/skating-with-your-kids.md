The story that went around on Good Morning America a while back — a mother who picked up skateboarding at 43 specifically to spend more time with her son — struck a nerve because it's a situation more parents are recognising.

Your kid skates, or wants to skate, or has been bugging you about going to the park. You used to skate, or you've always been curious, or you've been looking for an excuse. The idea of learning alongside them is appealing. The execution feels uncertain.

This post is for that situation.

## The Ability Gap: What to Expect

Children learn physical skills faster than adults. This is biological, not motivational — their nervous systems are more plastic, their bodies recover faster from the repetition required to build skill, and they carry less fear of falling.

Your kid will almost certainly outpace you. If they're already skating, they may already be significantly ahead of where you'll be for months. This can feel embarrassing. It doesn't have to.

A few things that help:

**Name it early.** Tell your child honestly that they're going to be better at this faster than you. Make it explicit and low-stakes: "You're going to be ahead of me, and that's going to be obvious, and that's completely fine." Kids who understand that their parent has real limitations, rather than watching the parent struggle to pretend otherwise, generally handle the dynamic better.

**Find your own goals, separate from theirs.** If your child is working on kickflips and you're working on feeling comfortable pushing, you're not competing. You're both doing skating, just at very different points. This is fine and actually models something useful about process goals.

**Let them teach you.** Teenagers and pre-teens respond surprisingly well to being asked to explain something they know. Inviting your child to show you how to do something — and genuinely trying to learn from the explanation — shifts the dynamic from parent-struggles-while-child-watches to genuine exchange.

## The Pressure Trap

The cautionary version of parent-child skating is the "skate parent" phenomenon: adults who got back into skating and then inadvertently turned every session into coaching their child, expressing frustration at a kid's lack of progress, or living vicariously through their kid's skating in ways that stop being fun quickly.

The distinguishing feature of good family skating: you're there to skate, not to manage your child's skating. Your job at the park is to be on your own board, working on your own thing, and available if asked. Not hovering. Not directing. Not comparing your kid to other kids.

The moments that create genuinely good family skating memories are almost never the moments when someone lands something impressive. They're the ordinary session moments — pushing together, both falling on the same patch of rough pavement, sitting on the edge of a ramp watching other skaters, the five-minute conversation that happens while you're both catching your breath.

## Practical Session Structure

When skating together with a child, a structure that tends to work:

**Warm-up together.** Fifteen minutes of just pushing and carving in the same area. No agenda, no instruction. Get the feeling of being on the board back before anything else.

**Separate to your own practice.** Each person skates whatever they're working on. This can be in the same general area of the park — proximity without constant interaction. You're each in your own process.

**Reconvene occasionally.** Not on a schedule — just naturally, when one of you has something to show the other, or when you both take a break at the same time.

**Leave before anyone's done.** The classic mistake in family outings is staying until someone's exhausted, frustrated, or hurt. End the session when it's still good.

## If You're a Complete Beginner

If your child is already skating and you're starting from scratch, there's a specific challenge: your child's instinct will often be to jump ahead to "let me show you this trick" when what you actually need is thirty minutes of standing on a board.

A few options:

**Get a lesson first, without your child present.** One or two sessions with a skate instructor — even informally at a calm park — before you show up with your kid gives you a baseline that makes the joint session less lopsided.

**Find a family-oriented session.** Many parks have beginner hours or family-specific sessions where the terrain and pace suit new adults. Skating together in that context is easier than showing up during peak teen session.

**Use your kid as your support crew, not your instructor.** There's a difference between "Can you just skate nearby so I feel less alone?" and "Can you teach me to drop into that bowl?" The first is something most kids will happily do. The second puts them in a role they're not equipped for.

## The Upside Nobody Talks About

The parents who come back to skating alongside their kids consistently report something that's hard to anticipate: it changes the relationship in ways that extend well beyond the skatepark.

Skating together creates a shared language and a shared reference. You both know what it feels like to try something, fail at it, and try again. You've both sat on the edge of a ramp watching other skaters figure something out. You've both had the session where nothing worked and the session where everything clicked.

That shared physical experience is relatively rare in parent-child relationships past a certain age, and it turns out to be valuable. Not because of anything profound — just because you're doing something together that's genuinely difficult, genuinely fun, and genuinely mutual.

Get on the board. Let them be ahead of you. It's fine.

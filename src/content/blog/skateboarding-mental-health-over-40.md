When I came back to skating at 46, I told myself it was exercise. That felt like a reasonable justification — lower impact than running, better for my knees than basketball, something I could do on my own schedule.

I wasn't wrong. But I was underselling it.

What skating actually gave me back, more than fitness, was a reliable way to exit my own head for ninety minutes. The specific kind of focus that skating demands — where you have to be present on the board, on the surface, on what's in front of you — doesn't leave much room for whatever you were worrying about before you arrived.

Research has caught up to what skaters have known for years. The evidence is worth looking at.

## The Exeter University Study

In 2021, the University of Exeter published research examining how skateboarding affects adults navigating depression and anxiety in midlife. The study wasn't focused on elite athletes or competitive skating — it was looking at ordinary adults at community skateparks.

What it found: regular skating was associated with meaningful reductions in self-reported depression and anxiety. The mechanisms weren't just cardiovascular. The study identified several specific factors:

**Social belonging.** Skate parks create informal social structures where adults interact across age groups without the usual social scripts. You don't need to explain yourself or your life situation — you're just someone who skates.

**Mastery and progression.** Learning something physical as an adult — something difficult, with visible progress — provides a type of confidence-building that most adult life doesn't offer. You can measure your improvement. That's rare.

**Child-parent bonding.** Among parents in the study, skating alongside children created a specific kind of relationship that was hard to replicate in other contexts.

**Flow states.** The focus required to skate effectively is incompatible with rumination. You cannot worry about your mortgage and execute a carve turn simultaneously.

## The NIH Heart Rate Data

A 2020 study published in the National Institutes of Health database tracked adults skating at community parks and measured their cardiovascular output throughout sessions.

The findings were striking: average heart rate across sessions was 138.2 bpm — 71.7% of predicted maximum. Around 70% of each session was spent at moderate or vigorous intensity. Average session length was 65 minutes.

This is a significant cardiovascular workout by any clinical measure. For context, the standard recommendation for cardiovascular health improvement is 150 minutes of moderate-intensity exercise per week. A single skating session covers a meaningful portion of that — without feeling like a workout, because it isn't experienced as one.

That psychological difference matters. The primary reason adults abandon exercise programs is that they're unpleasant. Skating, when it's going well, is the opposite of unpleasant.

## Why Middle Age Specifically

The benefits of skating for mental health don't appear to be age-neutral. There's something about the combination of physical challenge, novelty, and social environment that hits differently when you're 42 than when you're 22.

At 22, you're likely to have multiple contexts for mastery, novelty, and social connection. Work is new. Relationships are forming. The world feels manageable.

At 45, those sources often narrow. Work becomes routine. Social circles contract. Physical capability feels like it's declining rather than growing. The combination creates a specific psychological vulnerability that skating — with its constant small challenges, its visible progression, its informal community — addresses directly.

The research doesn't claim skating is a substitute for clinical mental health care. But it does suggest it's a more powerful tool than we typically give it credit for.

## What the First Session Actually Does

There's a chemical explanation for why a skating session reliably improves mood, even on a day when you fall a lot.

Physical exertion at moderate-to-vigorous intensity triggers endorphin release — well-established. But skating also involves repeated pattern recognition, problem-solving, and small achievement (landing a carve without wobbling, holding a line through a turn, making it through a rough section without bailing). Each small success triggers a dopamine response.

The combination of endorphins from the physical effort and dopamine from the skill execution creates a biochemical state that is, simply, good. It's not complicated. But it's real, and it's repeatable.

## On Feeling Ridiculous

One thing the research can't quantify but anyone who's come back to skating can confirm: there is a specific joy in doing something that looks slightly ridiculous, that your colleagues would raise an eyebrow at, and that is entirely yours.

Middle-aged life can become very serious very fast. Job, family, mortgage, health — everything matters, everything has consequences. Skating, at least for the 90 minutes you're doing it, matters in a completely different way. You fall, you laugh (or swear), you get back up. The stakes are low. That is not a minor thing.

## Practical Notes

If you're returning to skating specifically because you're struggling with anxiety or depression, a few things are worth knowing:

**Consistency beats intensity.** Three 45-minute sessions a week will do more for your mood than one two-hour session. The regularity is the medicine.

**The awkward phase is worth pushing through.** The first month of coming back will not feel like the mental health win I'm describing. You'll be frustrated, you'll fall, your body will ache. The benefits accumulate after you get past the initial learning curve.

**Community helps.** Skating alone is fine. Skating with other adults who skate is notably better. The social element is part of what makes it effective. Find your people.

**Early morning sessions hit differently.** Starting your day with 45 minutes of skating — before the inbox, before the commute, before the decisions — sets a biochemical baseline that most other morning routines don't match.

The research says what regular skaters already know. The board is good for your head. Pick it back up.
